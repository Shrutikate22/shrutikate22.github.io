The Science of Coffee: From Bean to Brew

A 15-minute microlesson that turns extraction chemistry into something you actually want to learn — built with six interactive block types in Rise 360.

01 — The Challenge

Make science sticky without a classroom

Most "fun topic" eLearning is just decorated text. I wanted to prove that even a casual subject like coffee could be built with real instructional rigor — needs analysis, deliberate interaction design, and measurable outcomes — while still being something you'd actually enjoy clicking through.

The constraint: build it entirely in Rise 360 (no Storyline, no custom code) and make it portfolio-ready in under a week.

The real design challenge wasn't the content — it was proving that Rise's built-in blocks can carry genuine instructional weight when you sequence them with intention.

02 — Design Thinking

Starting with behavior, not content

Before touching Rise, I mapped the lesson around one question: what should the learner be able to DO after this? Not "know about extraction" — but troubleshoot a bad cup and fix it. That's a behavioral outcome, and it shaped every design decision.

Mapping the learning objectives before opening Rise
Storyboarding interaction types to objectives
1

Defined behavioral objectives first

Not "understand extraction" but "diagnose and fix a sour, bitter, or weak cup." This drove the scenario block in Section 5.

2

Matched each objective to a Rise block type

Tabs for comparison (grind/temp/time). Sorting for classification (flavor → extraction zone). Scenario for application.

3

Sequenced from concrete to abstract

Start with something physical (the coffee cherry), build to the science (extraction), then apply it (troubleshooting). Cognitive load stays managed.

4

Designed the knowledge check last

Assessment questions written directly from the objectives — not from the content. If it's not in the objectives, it's not in the quiz.

03 — The Build

Six interaction types, one scroll

The lesson uses labeled graphics, tabs, flashcards, a sorting activity, a branching scenario, and a knowledge check — all native Rise blocks. No workarounds, no Storyline embeds. The goal was to show maximum instructional range within Rise's constraints.

Labeled graphic: coffee cherry anatomy
Tabs: grind, temp, and time variables
Flashcards: five brew methods compared

The branching scenario is the centerpiece. The learner brews a pour over, something goes wrong, and they diagnose it. Three branches — sour, bitter, or weak — each with targeted feedback tied directly to the extraction science taught earlier. It's Kirkpatrick Level 2 in a scroll.

04 — Visual Strategy

Moody, not corporate

I deliberately avoided the bright, flat stock-photo look that plagues most eLearning. The visual language is warm, dark, and tactile — espresso browns, cream highlights, moody photography. It should feel like a specialty coffee shop, not a compliance module.

05 — What I'd Do Differently

Honest reflection

Rise's scenario block is functional but shallow — you get one decision point, not true multi-path branching. If I rebuilt this, I'd embed a Storyline scenario for the troubleshooting section to allow deeper decision trees with cumulative consequences.

I'd also add spaced retrieval — a follow-up micro-quiz pushed 48 hours later — to test retention beyond the initial session. The knowledge check at the end measures immediate recall, not learning transfer.

The biggest lesson: Rise's constraints forced better design decisions. When you can't over-engineer, you have to be precise about which interaction earns its place. Every block had to justify itself against a learning objective.
