Delivering Feedback That Sticks

Most leaders give feedback. Few give feedback that actually changes behavior. This module shows you the difference — and gives you a model you can use in your next 1:1.

1 of 5 Why This Matters

Feedback is a leadership skill — not a management task.

Most leaders treat feedback as something to get through. High-trust leaders treat it as one of their most powerful behavior-change tools.

69% of employees say they'd work harder if they felt their efforts were better recognized
57% prefer corrective feedback over praise alone when it helps them improve
2.7× more likely to be engaged when managers give regular, meaningful feedback

2 of 5 The Model

S·B·I: The feedback framework that works.

Vague feedback creates defensiveness. The SBI model makes feedback specific, observable, and actionable — which is what changes behavior.

S

Situation — Anchor it in time and place

"In yesterday's team sync..." or "During the Q3 planning session last week..." Specificity removes ambiguity and defensiveness.

B

Behavior — Describe what you observed, not what you interpreted

"You interrupted three people before they finished their point" — not "You were dismissive." Stick to what anyone in the room could have seen.

I

Impact — Explain the effect it had

"It meant two ideas didn't get heard, and one team member went quiet for the rest of the meeting." Impact connects behavior to consequences — and that's what motivates change.

💡 Then stop talking. After delivering SBI, pause. Ask: "What's your reaction to that?" Silence is not failure — it means they're processing.

3 of 5 Common Mistakes

What separates feedback that lands from feedback that backfires.

The difference is usually in the language. Here's what to say — and what to drop.

✓ Say This
  • "In Monday's standup, you cut off two teammates before they finished."
  • "I noticed the report was submitted 2 days late without a heads-up."
  • "When you reframed the problem, the whole team shifted — that was exactly the right move."
  • "What's your read on how that went?"
✗ Avoid This
  • "You always talk over people."
  • "You need to be more accountable."
  • "Good job lately, keep it up."
  • "Everyone has noticed this."

The "always/never" trap: Words like "always" and "never" trigger defensiveness immediately — even when the feedback is accurate. Replace them with specific, dated observations every time.

Key Takeaways

Use SBI: Situation, Behavior, Impact — in that order, every time.
Describe observable behavior — not your interpretation of character or intent.
After delivering feedback, pause. Ask for their reaction. Silence is not failure.
Drop "always" and "never." Use specific, dated observations instead.
Feedback that earns trust is candid, specific, and respectful — all three at once.
