Onboarding Quest: Your First Day

A gamified microlearning experience with interactive challenges, drag-and-drop mechanics, and streak tracking.

Play the Game ↗

The Challenge

Onboarding is often the first learning experience at a new job — and for many employees, it's a forgettable one. Click-through slide decks covering company policies, org charts, and tool overviews don't create engagement or retention. New hires complete them to check a box and forget the content by lunch.

The goal was to reimagine Day 1 onboarding as something employees would actually enjoy — while still covering essential knowledge about tools, team structure, and company policies.

My Approach

  • Gamification framework: Designed three distinct challenges, each targeting a different onboarding knowledge area, with points, streaks, and badge rewards
  • Active recall over passive reading: Every interaction requires the learner to do something — drag, match, answer under time pressure — not just read
  • Custom code: Built entirely in HTML/CSS/React to demonstrate that engaging learning experiences don't require expensive authoring tools
  • Feedback loops: Immediate feedback on every interaction with positive reinforcement (confetti, streak counters) to sustain motivation
  • Progressive difficulty: Challenges start simple and build complexity, maintaining flow state

Design Decisions

Why gamification?

Onboarding content is inherently low-stakes — learners aren't intrinsically motivated to memorize an org chart. Gamification creates extrinsic motivation (points, streaks, badges) that sustains engagement long enough for the content to stick. The key is that the game mechanics serve the learning, not the other way around — every interaction maps to a real onboarding knowledge objective.

Why custom code instead of Storyline?

Two reasons. First, I wanted to demonstrate that instructional designers who can code have more creative freedom — the drag-and-drop, timed rounds, and confetti animations would be difficult to replicate in a traditional authoring tool. Second, this serves as a portfolio piece that shows range: I can work in Storyline, Rise, and custom code depending on what the project needs.

What I Learned

Gamification is easy to do poorly. Early iterations had too many game elements — leaderboards, timers on everything, bonus rounds — that distracted from the learning content. The final version stripped back to three core mechanics (points, streaks, badges) that directly reinforce the knowledge objectives without turning the experience into a game that happens to have onboarding content in it.

The biggest design lesson: every game mechanic should answer "what does this help the learner remember?" If it doesn't have a clear answer, cut it.
