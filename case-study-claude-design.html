The Problem

AI tools like Claude Design have made it possible to generate interactive learning modules in minutes. But most tutorials skip the part that actually determines whether training works: the instructional design process. The result is a growing flood of polished content that doesn't solve real performance problems.

When Tim Slade demonstrated building a Rise-like authoring tool with a single prompt, the ID community lit up. The conversation quickly became: "Are authoring tools dead?" But I thought the more important question was: "What happens when anyone can build a course — but not everyone knows what makes a course effective?"

My Approach

I created a comprehensive guide that treats AI as a production accelerator, not a replacement for design thinking. The guide walks through the full ADDIE-informed process — with exact Claude prompts at every step — showing how an instructional designer's expertise becomes more valuable, not less, when the tools get faster.

  • Process-first structure: Every phase starts with the ID thinking, then shows how Claude executes it
  • Ready-to-use prompts: 16 detailed prompts that readers can adapt to their own projects immediately
  • Design rationale: Each step explains why it matters, not just how to do it — connecting the prompt to the instructional principle behind it
  • Measurement built in: The guide doesn't stop at "deploy" — it includes a full Kirkpatrick L1–4 evaluation framework

Design Decisions

Why a process guide instead of a tutorial?

Tutorials show you what buttons to click. Process guides show you how to think. The ID community doesn't need another "how to use Claude" walkthrough — they need a framework for integrating AI into their existing design practice without sacrificing instructional quality. That's the gap this guide fills.

Why include measurement?

Most AI + learning content stops at "look what I built." That's the production mindset. An instructional designer's value is in asking "did it work?" Including Kirkpatrick L1–4 evaluation makes the point that the guide embodies: the thinking matters more than the tool.

Why now?

Tim Slade's Claude Design demo sparked a critical conversation in the ID community about the future of authoring tools and the role of the instructional designer. I saw an opportunity to contribute a perspective grounded in learning science — not to argue against AI, but to show how design expertise and AI capability amplify each other.

Impact

The LinkedIn post that sparked this guide — where I built on Tim Slade's Claude Design demo — received direct engagement from Tim himself, who commented: "You're asking the EXACT right questions!" The post positioned me at the center of a trending conversation in the ID community about the intersection of AI and instructional design.

The guide now lives as both a portfolio piece demonstrating my design process and a practical resource for other instructional designers navigating the AI shift.

What I Learned

Creating this guide reinforced something I've believed throughout my career: the most valuable skill in instructional design isn't knowing how to use a tool — it's knowing when and why to use it. AI didn't change that. It just made it more obvious.

I also learned that contributing to industry conversations — not just consuming them — is one of the most powerful ways to demonstrate expertise. Responding to Tim's demo with a thoughtful, structured perspective did more for my professional visibility than any application I've submitted.
